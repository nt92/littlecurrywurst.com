Kitchen stocked and fear of boiling water conquered, the next decision is what fruit to can (we‚Äôre not dealing with canning veggies because those are better frozen anyway). Pectin is high in fruits like apples, plums and grapes and lower in stony fruits like peaches, apricots and figs. Speaking of fruit, sugar, lemon juice and pectin…those are the lone ingredients for homemade jam. The steps in canning are multivariate and differ between recipe and source, but the basic layout2 is below:

  1. Place your mason jars on the canning rack inside the giant pot and bring to a simmer. Maintain the soft simmer while you‚Äôre preparing and boiling the jam.   2. Mash ~1 pound of your desired fruit and add to another large pot. Whisk in a few tablespoons of lemon juice and a pat of butter. Bring to a gentle boil.

#WurstTip: The lemon juice brings acidity and flavor to the jam mixture. Without it, the pectin won‚Äôt gel properly or would take longer than normal.

  1. Once boiling, mix in a shit ton of sugar (around 3 cups but it depends on the recipe) and increase the heat even more. The Pioneer Woman describes this state as a ‚Äúviolent boil,‚Äù but basically you want to see bubbles doing the ballet and spilling over the side.

  2. Boil for a few minutes (again, this varies from one minute to twenty) and then turn off the heat. A ‚Äúskin‚Äù should form on the surface, but this can be skimmed off with a spoon.

  3. Remember, your canning gear is still simmering in the background and probably making your kitchen a hot house. Remove the cans using the tongs and place on a clean surface.

  4. Pour the piping hot jam into the jars using the funnel. Leave a healthy ¬º inch between the jam and jar rim (called ‚Äúheadspace‚Äù).

  5. Clean the rims and place the lids/screw bands on the jar (which is going to be steaming, so be careful).

  6. Once all the jars are filled, place them into the canning rack in the giant pot and bring to a boil for ~10-15 minutes. This is killing microbes and any other wigglies that could potentially kill you.

  7. Take out the jars and let them set at room temperature for up to 24 hours. I‚Äôve heard whispers that during this incubation period, the jar lids begin to Pop! and create a canning symphony…my Post Jam Papers will include a recording of this music if the rumors are true.
